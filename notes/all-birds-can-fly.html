All Birds Can Fly

January 2024

"All birds can fly." is an easy example to start learning rule languages. Marvin Minsky used it in 1985 at AAAI.

canFly(X) :- bird(X) .

Except that some birds can't fly. But that's not a problem, circumscription can be used.

canFly(X) :- bird(X), ~abnormal(X, canFly) .
bird(X) :- penguin(X) .
abnormal(X, canFly) :- penguin(X) .

But then, how and where is the assertion made that some thing is a bird? Rules that define a concept must admit no exception. For instance, a bird always has feathers.

bird(X) :- has(X, feathers) .

But what about a single feather then?

bird(X) :- has(X, E), envelope(E), isComposedOf(E, F), feather(F) .
feather(X) :- neural_network(X, PictureOfX) .

The first rule above is most easily expressed in a Description Logic syntax. The second rule illustrates an example of neural-symbolic integration.

OK, now, what about "can fly"? Flying is itself a concept, "can fly" shouldn't be an atomic predicate. Let's rather introduce a modality.

◊fly(X) :- bird(X) .

But what about "flying" then? Flying is a state of affairs. At the same time, it a temporal entity that has a beginning and an end.

temporalEntity(A) :- fly(A) .
beginsAt(A, _) :- temporalEntity(A) .
endsAt(A, _) :- temporalEntity(A) .

Anything else?
